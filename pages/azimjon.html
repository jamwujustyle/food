Autobiography

Hello) I am Azimjon from Food Language and Identity. My name is Azimjon. I am senior Computer Science student at Webster. Despite technical nature of my major, I’ve always been interested in how food connects people to their culture and identity. After some time, I noticed how meals were more than just something to eat, they carried meaning, stories, and a sense of who we actually are. When I started learning about globalization and cultural exchange, I figured out that food travels and changes just like language does. One dish can show both history and adaptation, depending on who makes it and where.

In addition, my personal academic background helps me to see the foodways not just in traditions, I see them as systems of communication and even power. I enjoy studying or learning how recipes, taste, and cooking practices reveal something about society and relationships between people. Also, I like to cooking myself. For me it feels like a small act of keeping memory alive while at the same time also experimenting with new things. Overall, I see food as a way to understand both personal and global connections, and that’s why I’m drawn to studying it deeper.

Key Learnings

From this course, I’ve learned that food is simply nutrition, it’s a form of power, identity, and history. Some theories such as Bourdieu’s ideas of taste and Mintz’s work on sugar show and depict how food marks social class and historical exploitation. These readings helped me to understand that what people like eating and how they describe it reflects their position in systems of power and globalization.

Personal Motto

“Food is a language of belonging.” For me, it means that food speaks even when any sort of words fail. It tells who we are and where we come from, and it can also show how we find belonging in new places.

Applying Course Themes

I am planning to use what I learned about food, language, and identity in future projects or maybe even research. I’m especially very interested in how digital spaces can help preserve traditional food knowledge and share it across communities. Also, through writing, interviews, or storytelling, I want to explore how food can both protect culture and challenge inequality at the same time.

"Food is memory mode tangible, tradition mode taste-able."
