Research & Methodology

An academic exploration of how food serves as a vessel for cultural memory, identity preservation, and the migrant experience.

Topic Introduction

Under the circumstances of globalization and migration, food is a marker of identity as a portable home of diasporic populations. I would like to concentrate on the cultural, linguistic and identity implications of the Uzbek cuisine to Uzbek migrants, and how the practices are mediated through the prism of class, gender, power and globalization. Our course on food and identity is pertinent to this topic as it connects foodways, language and cultural identity. Learning how the Uzbek food traditions are preserved, changed or modified in migrant settings, and how language related to food as names of dishes, labels, transliteration, descriptions on menus interrelates with identity, we learn how food language is used as a means of continuity and transformation of cultural practices

Historical Development

Uzbekistan cuisine is the product of a very long history of Silk Road trade, nomadic pastoralism, and Soviet collectivization combining the Central Asian grain and lamb with Persian, Russian and Turkic influences. According to such scholars as Sidney Mintz, food systems reflect culture and history: the foods of other peoples would become bright signifier of difference, carriers of belonging and nonbelonging.

In the Uzbek communities overseas such as in Russia, Germany, the U.S., migrants recreated the versions of the canonical dishes like plov, samsa, lagman by adapting them to the availability of ingredients, local preferences, and regulatory policies of the host countries. Simultaneously, the linguistics surrounding these dishes also change - the immigrant restaurants may offer either Uzbek plov or pilaf in order to suit the needs of both national and foreign customers. Migration networks, global food chains and digital media also influence the processes of Uzbek food identity travel, hybridisation and marketing. Food eventually assists the diasporic Uzbeks to express continuity with the homeland as well as adaptation in the new cultures.

Sociocultural & Sociopolitical Context

The food and language of the diasporic Uzbek cuisine in relation to power, class, gender and identity intersect in several ways. First, class: Economic resources might determine access to traditional ingredients in host countries; the authentic restaurants of Uzbek culture might focus on higher-end customers, commercializing heritage food as a tourist destination or culinary tourism. Second, gender: in Uzbek foodways, women tend to do the cooking and host guests, thus the cooking practices of the diaspora are genderised labour and reproduced identities who cooks, who teaches younger generations. Third, race and ethnicity: Uzbek migrant food enterprises can serve as both a social mobility and a place of cultural negotiation, such as when the Uzbeks in Russia are discriminated against, their restaurants can become a cultural center, as well as the preservation of language and identity. Language is involved: menu design, transliteration, bilingual signage both negotiate identity Uzbek vs Russian, sometimes even English. Food, therefore, becomes a belonging and non-belonging tool - what is defined as the real Uzbek and the Uzbek-Russian fusion, demonstrates social stratifications. In addition, the globalization and diaspora imply that food may be othered, commodified; this is associated with the cultural imperialism and homogenization theory, in which local tradition comes into conflict with international food chains.

Altogether, the discussion of diasporic Uzbek cuisine in the light of these perspectives will show that food-language practices reproduce and challenge the cultural power relations, identity work, and belonging.

Global Reception

Migration and globalization have altered the ways in which cuisines are being perceived and adapted globally. Traditional home meals like Uzbek plov, Filipino adobo or Indian biryani are now served all over the world as a sign of cultural pride and as a sign of adaptation. Migrants find it common to make changes in recipes so as to obtain new settings- changing ingredients, changing techniques, and meaning. The change proves that food is a language of adaptation and a combination of memory and innovation (Duruz, 2016).

Nonetheless, authenticity may also be corrupted by popularity all around the world. The cuisines of migrants are often commercialised or borrowed away without any cultural meaning (Hooks, 1992). However, migrants assert control again via storytelling, family cookbooks and social media as they give an account of their food culture in their words (Heldke, 2003). Those stories enable migrant cooks to retain emotional memory and reach the international audience turning home cooking into a cultural intermediary and a way of fighting the homogenization.

Identifying the Gap

Even though there is abundant literature about migration and meals, it lacks a comprehensive discussion about the linguistic and emotional aspect of home cooking. There is a substantial number of studies on food as a symbol of identity or nostalgia but there are very few studies analyzing how language itself maintains the memory in a migrant kitchen. There are words such as osh, lagman or samsa that will never translate the meaning without the cultural richness (Sutton, 2001).

The way migrants engage in the use of bilingual or heritage language to cook and share recipes can be pursued as future research, and how the combination of verbal and culinary practices helps maintain the cultural belonging. A discussion of speech patterns, narrating and intergenerational teaching in kitchens may enhance our knowledge regarding the creation of identity by food and language in the context of diasporas.Appadurai, A. (1988). How to Make a National Cuisine: Cookbooks in Contemporary India. Comparative Studies in Society and History, 30(1), 3–24.

Positioning Your Perspective

Positioning Your Perspective Many scholars and writers say that food plays a powerful role in the lives of migrants. When people move to a new country, they often lose familiar places, languages, and traditions. But cooking food from home helps them remember where they came from. It becomes a way to keep their memories, identity, and family culture alive. Researchers often call this the “language of home cooking.” It is a language of comfort and belonging that does not need words. I agree with this view, but I also believe there is more to it. Cooking at home is not just about remembering the past — it is also about adapting to the present. Migrants often cannot find the same ingredients they used back home. Because of that, they learn to mix local and traditional ingredients and create new versions of old dishes. This shows that food can change, just like people do, and that identity is something living, not frozen in time. For example, an Uzbek family living in another country might not find the exact ingredients for plov, so they use what is available. The taste may be a little different, but the feeling and memory stay the same. The dish becomes a mix of memory and adaptation — a symbol of how culture continues to grow in new places. My perspective adds to academic debates by showing that migration does not destroy culture — it transforms it. Through home cooking, people connect their past and present, keeping traditions alive while also making space for change. Cooking becomes a daily act of identity, memory, and creativity. In my opinion, the language of home cooking is more than just recipes — it tells the story of who we are, where we come from, and how we find belonging in new homes.

Public Engagement

My research on the intersection of food, language, and identity has the potential to inform public and educational projects that promote cultural understanding and community engagement. One approach could be the development of interactive workshops or cooking classes that combine culinary instruction with storytelling. These workshops could allow participants to share family recipes, discuss the cultural history of dishes, and explore how food reflects personal and collective identities.

Another possibility is the creation of digital platforms or multimedia projects where migrant communities can document and share cooking practices, recipes, and food narratives. Such platforms could serve as educational tools for schools or community centers, helping young people understand cultural diversity, migration, and heritage through food.

Additionally, public lectures, cultural festivals, or collaborative school projects could incorporate my findings by emphasizing the role of food as a tool for empathy and communication. By connecting communities through shared culinary experiences, these initiatives could encourage cross-cultural dialogue, appreciation for traditions, and intergenerational learning.

Overall, my research highlights that food is not only sustenance but a powerful medium for learning, connection, and cultural preservation, which can be leveraged in both public and educational contexts to build understanding and celebrate diversity.
