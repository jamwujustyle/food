Case Study — KIMCHI, KIMJANG, AND CULINARY MEMORY IN THE KOREAN DIASPORA

Title: Kimchi-making (kimjang) as a Language of Memory among Korean Migrants

Overview (short):

Kimchi and the communal practice of kimjang (seasonal kimchi-making) function as an embodied language of memory for Korean migrant communities. Beyond nutrition, kimchi-making transmits family histories, preserves Korean linguistic practices, and recreates social bonds in diasporic settings. When migrants adapt ingredients and techniques in host countries, the practice still sustains emotional continuity and cultural identity.

Context & Background:

Kimchi is a traditional fermented vegetable dish central to Korean cuisine; kimjang — the large-scale seasonal preparation and sharing of kimchi — was inscribed on UNESCO’s Intangible Cultural Heritage list as a communal cultural practice that reinforces group identity and intergenerational transmission. In migration contexts (e.g., Korean communities in the United States, Europe, and elsewhere), kimjang and everyday kimchi-making become sites where memory, language (Korean speech, recipe-terms, kinship terms), and identity are enacted and passed on.

Key Findings:

  • Embodied memory: The sensory aspects of kimchi (smell, taste, texture) trigger family memories and act as non-verbal carriers of heritage.
  • Community spaces: Migrant women often create community kitchens or group kimjang events that recreate social life and provide a space for Korean-language use and storytelling.
  • Adaptation ≠ loss: Ingredient substitutions (local cabbage varieties, different peppers) are common, but adaptation itself becomes part of the migration narrative — recipes are altered yet meanings persist.
  • Cultural translation: Kimchi-making can operate as “culinary storytelling” for host societies, helping migrants communicate identity and history through food-sharing and small food businesses.

Reflection:

This case confirms that home cooking operates as a language of memory in migration contexts. Kimchi and kimjang are not only culinary practices but social technologies that preserve language, ritual, and belonging. In diaspora, the collective work of preparing kimchi reconstructs social networks and offers a medium for intergenerational teaching; children learn cultural vocabulary and family stories not only by hearing them, but by tasting and participating. Importantly, adaptation to local ingredients shows resilience rather than erosion — the modified recipe becomes a narrative of movement and survival. For our project topic — Migration, Memory, and the Language of Home Cooking — this example illustrates how food-making communicates cultural memory and how such practices might be studied both ethnographically and through public engagement (community workshops, oral histories, and culinary storytelling events).
